In the quiet hours of early spring, when the mist still clings to distant peaks and birds begin their morning chorus, I find myself returning to the classical poets. There's something about their economy of language, the way twenty characters can contain an entire world, that speaks to a truth beyond mere words.

The Tang poets understood this implicitly. When Meng Haoran woke on a spring morning, he didn't need elaborate description—just the essence of the moment, the sound of birds, the memory of rain, the fallen blossoms.

## The Complexity of Simplicity

But not all poetry works the same way. Some poems build their meaning through accumulation, through the patient layering of image upon image. Tao Yuanming, writing centuries earlier in the Jin Dynasty, often worked in longer lines, with translations that resist the compressed elegance of the Tang masters.

## Bilingual Considerations

When we present these poems bilingually, we face an interesting design challenge. The Chinese original, with its vertical columns reading right-to-left, occupies a completely different spatial logic than the English translation. The five-character lines fit naturally into narrow columns; the English sprawls horizontally, resistant to constraint.

## Conclusion

Typography is never neutral. The way we arrange text on a page shapes how we read it, what we notice, how we pause. For classical Chinese poetry, the vertical column isn't just decorative—it's part of the reading experience, a visual echo of the calligraphic tradition from which these poems emerged.

But that's a topic for another day. For now, I'll return to my books, to the pleasure of covering entire worlds without leaving my cottage.
